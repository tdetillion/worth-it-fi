If you want to build wealth, tax-advantaged retirement accounts are your most powerful tool. The difference between investing in a regular brokerage account and using a 401(k) or IRA isn't just a few percentage points—over decades, it can mean hundreds of thousands of dollars in your pocket instead of the IRS's.

This isn't complicated. You're already earning income and paying taxes on it. These accounts let you either delay those taxes or eliminate them entirely on your investment gains. That's huge.

What Are Tax-Advantaged Accounts?

Tax-advantaged accounts are investment accounts that get special treatment from the IRS. Instead of paying taxes on every dividend and capital gain along the way, your money grows either tax-deferred or tax-free.

There are two main types you need to know about:

401(k): An employer-sponsored retirement account. Your contributions come directly from your paycheck before taxes, and many employers will match a percentage of what you contribute. This is the closest thing to free money you'll find.

IRA (Individual Retirement Account): A retirement account you open yourself through a brokerage like Vanguard, Fidelity, or Schwab. You have more control over your investment options, and it's available whether or not your employer offers a 401(k).

401(k) Fundamentals

Your 401(k) contributions are deducted from your paycheck before income taxes are calculated. If you make $60,000 and contribute $6,000 to your 401(k), you only pay income tax on $54,000. That money then grows tax-deferred until you withdraw it in retirement.

Employer match is critical. If your employer offers to match 50% of your contributions up to 6% of your salary, that's an immediate 50% return on your money. There is no investment strategy that beats that. Always contribute at least enough to get the full employer match.

The contribution limit for 2024 is $23,000 per year. If you're 50 or older, you can contribute an additional $7,500 as a "catch-up contribution."

Roth 401(k) option: Some employers offer a Roth version of the 401(k). With a Roth 401(k), you pay taxes on your contributions now, but all future growth and withdrawals are tax-free. This can be valuable if you expect to be in a higher tax bracket in retirement or if you're early in your career with a relatively low income.

Traditional vs Roth 401(k)

Feature Traditional 401(k) Roth 401(k)
Contributions Pre-tax (reduces taxable income now) After-tax (no immediate tax benefit)
Tax Benefit Timing Now—lower taxes today Retirement—tax-free withdrawals

Best For Higher income now, expect lower tax bracket in retirement Lower income now, expect higher bracket later

Yes! If your employer offers both options, you can split your contributions between Traditional and Roth 401(k). The combined total cannot exceed the annual limit ($23,000 for 2024).

This can be a smart strategy—contributing to both gives you tax diversification. In retirement, you'll have some money that's already been taxed (Roth) and some that hasn't (Traditional), giving you flexibility.

You have several options when you leave:

Leave it: You can keep the money in your old employer's plan if the balance is over $5,000.

Roll it over: Move it to your new employer's 401(k) or to an IRA. This is often the best choice—IRAs typically have lower fees and better investment options.

Cash out: Don't do this unless absolutely necessary. You'll pay income taxes plus a 10% penalty if under 59½.

The general priority order is: (1) 401(k) up to employer match, (2) Max Roth IRA, (3) Then return to 401(k) up to the limit.
