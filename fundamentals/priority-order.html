Priority Order: Where To Put Your Money

You have limited money and unlimited places to put it. This is the optimal order to build wealth while managing risk. Follow these steps in order—each one builds on the last to create a solid financial foundation.

1

Build Starter Emergency Fund

$1,000 – $2,000

Why: Prevents small emergencies from becoming credit card debt. A car repair or medical bill won't derail your finances.

Where: High-yield savings account (separate from checking)

Then move to Step 2
2

Get Full Employer 401(k) Match

Typically 3-6% of salary

Why: This is free money—an immediate 50-100% return on your contribution. No investment beats that.

How much: Whatever percentage gets your full employer match. Check your benefits portal.

Learn more in 401(k) Basics

Then move to Step 3
3

Pay Off High-Interest Debt

Debt above 7% interest

Why: Debt above 7% is costing you more than investments typically earn. Paying it off is a guaranteed return.

Includes: Credit cards, personal loans, high-interest student loans

Method: Avalanche (highest interest first) for math optimization, or Snowball (smallest balance first) for psychological wins

Then move to Step 4
4

Build Full Emergency Fund

3-6 months of expenses

Why: Job loss protection and peace of mind. You can handle major setbacks without going into debt.

How much: 3 months if you have stable income and job security. 6 months if income is variable or job market is uncertain.

Learn more in Emergency Fund 101

Then move to Step 5
5

Max Out Roth IRA

$7,000/year (2024)

Why: Tax-free growth forever. More investment options and typically lower fees than most 401(k) plans.

Where: Open at Vanguard, Fidelity, or Schwab

Learn more in IRA Basics

Then move to Step 6
6

Pay Off Moderate-Interest Debt

Debt at 4-7% interest

Why: Now that you have a safety net and retirement savings started, knock out moderate debt.

Includes: Car loans, federal student loans, some private loans

Decision point: If debt is under 5%, some prefer to invest instead. Both approaches are valid—pick what lets you sleep at night.

Then move to Step 7
7

Max Out 401(k)

$23,000/year (2024)

Why: Additional tax-advantaged space for serious wealth building. Reduces your taxable income significantly.

Note: This is above and beyond the employer match you're already getting in Step 2.

Learn more in 401(k) Basics

Then move to Step 8
8

Invest in Taxable Brokerage

No limit

Why: You've maxed all tax-advantaged space—congratulations! Now build additional wealth in a regular investment account.

What: Low-cost index funds, same strategy as your retirement accounts

Bonus: More flexibility than retirement accounts—no penalties for early withdrawal (though you'll pay taxes on gains)

You've optimized your money allocation!

What if I can't afford all these steps?

Start where you are. Even $50/month toward Step 2 is progress. Here's how to make it work:

  • Increase contributions by 1% annually as your income grows
  • It's okay to make partial progress on multiple steps simultaneously
  • Focus on Steps 1-4 first—they provide the foundation
  • Something is always better than nothing

What about saving for a house, car, or other goals?

After Step 4 (full emergency fund), you have flexibility to split money between Step 5 and other savings goals. Here's the framework:

  • Short-term goals (under 3 years): Keep in high-yield savings—don't risk it in the market
  • Medium-term goals (3-10 years): Consider investing, but understand you might need to delay if market dips
  • House down payment: Many people pause at Step 5 to save for a home, then resume after purchase
