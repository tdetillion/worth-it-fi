Understanding Inflation

Why your money buys less over time—and what to do about it

What Is Inflation?

Inflation means your money buys less over time. It's the steady increase in prices across the economy.

Real-world examples:

  • A gallon of gas cost $0.65 in 1978, now it's ~$3.00
  • A cup of coffee was $2 in 2000, now it's $5+
  • A movie ticket was $5 in 1995, now it's $15+

Why it matters: If you're planning for financial independence 20, 30, or 40 years from now, you need to account for inflation. Otherwise, you'll think you need $1.2M when you actually need much more to maintain the same lifestyle.

The Power of Compound Inflation

Just like compound interest helps your investments grow, compound inflation works against your purchasing power. Even "low" inflation of 3% per year adds up dramatically over time.

How $100,000 loses purchasing power at 3% inflation:

After 10 years
$74,409
26% loss
After 20 years
$55,368
45% loss
After 30 years
$41,199
59% loss
After 40 years
$30,656
69% loss

The Rule of 72: Divide 72 by the inflation rate to see how long until your money loses half its value.

  • At 3% inflation: 72 ÷ 3 = 24 years
  • At 4% inflation: 72 ÷ 4 = 18 years

This is why cash sitting in a checking account is slowly losing value every year.

Why This Matters for Financial Independence

When planning for FI, both your expenses AND your portfolio need to account for inflation.

Example Scenario

Today: You need $4,000/month ($48,000/year) to live

FI target today: $48,000 × 25 = $1.2M portfolio

In 46 years at 3% inflation:

  • Monthly expenses will be $15,735 (same lifestyle, inflated dollars)
  • Annual expenses will be $188,815
  • Portfolio needed: $188,815 × 25 = $4.7M

The big numbers look scary, but here's the key insight: If your expenses inflate by 4x, and your portfolio inflates by 4x, your purchasing power stays the same. The 4% safe withdrawal rate still works.

This is why our FI calculator shows both "future dollars" and "today's dollars"—the math works, even though the numbers get large.

How to Protect Yourself from Inflation

The good news: There are proven ways to stay ahead of inflation.

Invest in stocks: The stock market has historically returned ~10% annually, well above the ~3% inflation rate. This is why investing beats saving in a checking account.

Don't keep all your money in cash: Emergency funds in high-yield savings accounts are fine (and necessary), but long-term wealth should be invested. Cash loses value every year to inflation.

Your salary typically keeps pace: Most employers give annual raises that roughly match inflation. If you're earning $60,000 today, you'll likely earn much more in 20 years—not because you're richer, but because dollars are worth less.

Social Security is inflation-adjusted: If you're counting on Social Security in retirement, it adjusts annually for inflation (called COLA - Cost of Living Adjustment).

Real estate can help: If you own a home with a fixed mortgage, your housing cost stays the same while everything else inflates. Your $2,000/month mortgage payment in 2025 will feel much cheaper in 2045.

The Bottom Line

Don't let inflation scare you away from long-term planning. Yes, the numbers get big over 30-40 years, but the math still works.

Key takeaways:

  • Inflation is real and unavoidable—plan for ~3% annually
  • Your expenses will inflate, but so will your income and investments
  • Investing in stocks historically outpaces inflation by ~7% per year
  • The 4% safe withdrawal rule accounts for inflation
  • Focus on "real" (inflation-adjusted) values, not nominal values

Remember: If you're 25 and planning for FI at 65, those big numbers (like a $4M portfolio) aren't actually bigger in terms of what they can buy—they're just reflecting 40 years of inflation. Your purchasing power remains the same.
